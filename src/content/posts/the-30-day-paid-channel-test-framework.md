Most new channel launches fail not because the channel was wrong, but because the test was wrong. Too short to get real signal. Too loose to isolate what caused the result. Too quick to call it a win before the learning phase ended, or too slow to cut when it wasn't working.

Thirty days is a useful forcing function. It's long enough to get past the algorithm's learning phase on most platforms, and short enough that if something isn't working, you haven't committed six months of budget to finding out. The key is treating those 30 days as a structured experiment with a predetermined hypothesis and a decision framework ready before you spend the first dollar.

We've run this process across every major paid channel—and for Agora's own DOOH (LED-wall) offering, it's exactly how we validated the format across [six different business verticals in the first month](/creative-testing-across-verticals-dooh/). Here's the framework.

## Why 30 Days?

The 30-day window maps to three real constraints.

**Platform learning phases.** Meta, Google, and most programmatic platforms run an initial learning phase where the algorithm is still finding its footing. On Meta, this typically runs 7–14 days. Cutting a test before it exits the learning phase means you're judging performance that was never meant to be final. Thirty days clears this margin on almost every major platform.

**Enough volume for signal.** A 10-day test might produce 30 conversions. That's not enough to know whether a 30% swing in performance is real or noise. Thirty days, at typical ad volumes, usually produces enough data to distinguish actual results from statistical luck.

**Short enough to correct.** Budget spent on a failing channel is sunk cost; budget saved by cutting a failing channel early is recovered. A 30-day ceiling keeps the downside bounded. If the channel isn't working by day 30, you have a clear answer, not a slowly bleeding line item.

## Phase 1: Setup (Days 1–5)

The test should be designed before you spend anything. Specifically:

**Define the single question you are testing.** Not "does this channel work?" but something falsifiable: "Can we acquire leads from [channel] at a CAC below $[X]?" or "Will awareness spend on [channel] lift branded search volume by [Y]%?" One question per test. Multiple objectives dilute every metric.

**Set the budget ceiling.** Decide the maximum you're willing to spend to get an answer, and treat it as a cap, not a target. A 30-day test should have a fixed budget so that you're not tempted to keep funding a poor performer hoping it will turn.

**Pick two metrics only.** A primary metric (the one that decides pass/fail) and a secondary metric (context). If you track 15 things you will find 15 conflicting stories. Choose the one number that answers your hypothesis and one supporting number that explains why.

**Build creative for the channel.** This is worth its own point because it's where most tests break. Reusing assets that performed on Meta for out-of-home, or copying Google display creative for paid social, produces results that tell you nothing about the channel—only that you ran unsuitable creative. Each channel has constraints; brief into them, not around them. [Creative testing across six verticals](/creative-testing-across-verticals-dooh/) showed us that the format's constraints matter more than the message's specifics.

## Phase 2: Launch and Baseline (Days 6–15)

The first ten days of live spend exist to establish a baseline, not to optimize. During this phase:

**Don't touch the setup.** Every change you make resets the learning phase on most platforms and makes it harder to isolate what's driving results. Log observations, but resist the urge to adjust. The algorithm needs runway.

**Watch for pacing problems.** Check that the budget is delivering roughly as planned. Severe underspend or overspend in the first week is a signal the targeting is too narrow or too broad, not that the channel is failing.

**Record the secondary metric.** If you're testing awareness with branded search lift as the secondary metric, pull your baseline branded search volume now so you have something to compare against at day 30.

The worst thing you can do in phase 2 is judge creative performance based on 5 days of data and start swapping assets. You'll never know what the original was actually going to do.

## Phase 3: Optimize (Days 16–25)

By the midpoint, the algorithm has stabilized and you have enough data to make meaningful adjustments. This is the only phase where active changes are warranted.

**Tighten or expand targeting.** If cost-per-result is running high, you may be reaching people too broadly. If it's running low and volume is thin, you may have under-targeted. Adjust once, then let it run.

**Test one creative variable.** If you have evidence that creative is holding back results, introduce one variation—same message, different format, or same format, different angle. Don't run four variants. You don't have enough volume to make four-way tests meaningful.

**Cross-reference against platform reporting.** Platform ROAS and platform-reported conversions are self-graded. If you're running a test where the goal is actual revenue or incremental leads, validate against your backend. [Platform-reported ROAS overstates results](/why-platform-reported-roas-overstates-results/) consistently—the advertiser who doesn't check their accounting system against their ad dashboard is measuring confidence, not performance. This matters even in a short test.

## Phase 4: Evaluate (Days 26–30)

On day 30, you have one question to answer: did the channel meet the hypothesis you set on day one?

Not "did it look promising?" Not "do we see potential?" Not "would more budget have helped?" The hypothesis either passed or it didn't.

**The three outcomes:**

| **Result** | **Decision** | **What it means** |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| Primary metric hit, CAC/CPL in range | Commit | Allocate a permanent budget; move to ongoing optimization |
| Primary metric close, clear learnings on what to fix | Iterate | Run a second 30-day test with the specific fix applied |
| Primary metric missed, no clear improvement path | Kill | Reallocate budget; revisit in 6 months if circumstances change |

The "iterate" path is where most teams get stuck. Iterate means you have a specific hypothesis about what will fix the result—"our creative wasn't channel-native" or "we targeted too broadly in week one." It doesn't mean "let's try again and see what happens." If you can't name the specific change, the right call is to kill.

## The Attribution Problem in Channel Tests

One thing to prepare for: attribution is messier in a channel test than in your steady-state campaigns. A new channel is likely not your final touchpoint, which means last-click attribution will undercount its contribution. This is especially true for awareness-oriented channels.

Before the test ends, decide how you'll account for this. Options include a holdout group (run the test in one geo or segment, compare against a control with no new channel spend), or a longer attribution window that captures the delayed conversion. Neither is perfect, but both are better than reading platform-reported numbers at face value. [Attribution model choice](/attribution-models-compared-spend-to-revenue/) matters most when your new channel sits early in the funnel—the model that serves a closing channel badly understates the role of an awareness one.

## What You Build When You Do This Right

A 30-day test, run properly, produces something more valuable than one data point: it produces a repeatable testing process. The second channel test you run this way will be better than the first, because you'll know where the setup broke, where the optimization phase was too aggressive, and what the evaluation actually required.

More importantly, it builds a portfolio of tested channels rather than a single all-in-bet. The [channel launch framework](/how-to-launch-a-new-paid-advertising-channel/) we use starts with exactly this structure—define the job, set a capped budget, build native creative, measure the one thing that answers the hypothesis. The 30-day test is its natural complement: the duration that makes those principles operational rather than aspirational.
